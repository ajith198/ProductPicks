If you already own a tent, a sleeping bag, and a headlamp from past trips, a stove is usually the next thing on the list — and it's easy to overspend on one before you know whether backpacking is a hobby you'll stick with. The good news is that a perfectly capable first stove costs less than a pair of trail runners, and the category hasn't changed much in years, so there's no risk of buying something that's obsolete by next season.

This guide covers what actually matters when picking a first backpacking stove, plus specific models worth considering. We haven't personally tested every unit listed here — we've researched published specs, pricing, and coverage from outlets like GearJunkie, CleverHiker, and OutdoorGearLab, and we'll flag anywhere we're relying on that rather than hands-on use.

## What Actually Matters in a First Backpacking Stove

### Fuel Type: Canister Is the Easy Starting Point

Nearly every beginner-friendly stove on the market screws directly onto a threaded isobutane-propane canister — the small green or yellow fuel cans sold at any outdoor retailer. Canister stoves are simple: screw it on, turn the valve, light it. Liquid-fuel (white gas) stoves burn better in extreme cold and at altitude, and alcohol stoves are lighter still, but both come with a steeper learning curve around priming, spilling, and burn control. Unless you already know you're heading into winter mountaineering territory, a canister stove is the right place to start.

### Boil Time and Simmer Control

Most canister stoves will boil 1 liter of water somewhere between 3 and 4.5 minutes under normal conditions — fine for rehydrating a dehydrated meal or making coffee. If you plan to actually cook (simmering rice, sauces, eggs) rather than just boiling water, look specifically for a stove with a wide flame-adjustment range; some ultralight stoves are built almost entirely for fast boiling and simmer poorly, running either roaring or off.

### Wind Resistance

An unprotected flame loses a huge amount of efficiency in even a light breeze, which is the single biggest complaint about the cheapest stoves. Some models integrate a wind-resistant burner head or come with a built-in windscreen; with others you'll want to pack a separate foil windscreen or cook in a sheltered spot. If you expect to cook above treeline or in exposed campsites often, weigh this more heavily than boil-time specs.

### Weight and Packed Size

Beginner canister stoves generally run 2.5-3.5 ounces and fold down small enough to fit inside your cook pot along with a canister. At this weight class, the difference between models is small enough that it shouldn't be the deciding factor over reliability and price — unless you're chasing an ultralight base weight specifically.

### Piezo Igniter vs. Lighter

Many stoves include a built-in push-button piezo igniter, which is convenient but can fail with age or a hard knock. Bring a lighter or matches as backup regardless of what your stove has built in — this is standard practice even among experienced backpackers, not just a beginner precaution.

## A Few Things to Verify Before You Buy

- Fuel canisters are sold separately and **cannot be shipped by air in checked baggage** — plan to buy them locally at your destination or at an outdoor retailer near the trailhead.
- Check the stove's **packed size against your cook pot** — most are designed to nest inside a small pot for storage, but not all pots and stoves pair well.
- If you'll often cook in exposed, windy spots, prioritize **wind resistance** over raw boil-time specs, or budget for a separate foil windscreen.
- Bring a **backup lighter or matches** regardless of whether the stove has a built-in igniter — piezo igniters are a common failure point over time.
- Look up the **current price directly on the retailer's page** — these fluctuate with sales more than the list prices above suggest.
