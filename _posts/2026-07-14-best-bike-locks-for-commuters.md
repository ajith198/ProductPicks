A bike lock only has one job, and most cheap ones fail at it within seconds. If you lock your bike outside every workday — at a train station, a bike rack, a street sign — you're not protecting it from an opportunist testing handles; you're protecting it from someone who came prepared, and increasingly that means a cordless angle grinder rather than bolt cutters. The lock that seemed like overkill five years ago is closer to the baseline now.

This guide covers what actually separates a real commuter lock from a false sense of security, plus specific models worth considering across price points. We haven't tested every lock listed here ourselves — we've researched published security ratings, independent lab test results, and owner feedback, and we'll flag anywhere we're relying on that rather than hands-on testing.

## What Actually Matters in a Commuter Bike Lock

### Lock Type: U-Lock vs. Chain vs. Folding vs. Cable

A **U-lock (D-lock)** is the standard recommendation for most commuters: a rigid hardened-steel shackle that resists prying and leverage attacks better than anything else at a comparable weight. The tradeoff is fixed interior space — you often can't loop both wheels and the frame without a separate cable, and an oddly shaped rack can make it hard to fit the shackle at all.

A **chain lock** trades some weight for flexibility — hardened links wrap around irregular street furniture and can secure both wheels plus the frame in one motion. The catch is real: a chain heavy enough to resist cutting is often 2+ kilograms, which is why brands like Hiplok sell wearable carry systems (a chain that clips around your waist) rather than expecting you to stuff it in a bag every day.

A **folding lock** — hardened steel plates joined by rivets that fold flat into a frame-mounted case — sits in between on both weight and security. The rivet joints are historically the weak point, and ratings vary a lot model to model; one well-known folding lock (see the ABUS Bordo note below) was actually downgraded by independent testers after retesting, which is a useful reminder to check the current rating rather than the marketing copy.

A **cable lock** is light, flexible, and cut in seconds with basic tools. Every buying guide we found agrees on this: use a cable only as a *secondary* lock for a wheel or saddle, never as your only lock.

### Security Ratings: What Sold Secure and ART Actually Mean

**Sold Secure** is an independent UK lab that rates locks Bronze, Silver, Gold, or the newer top tier, Diamond, based on how long the lock resists real attack tools in testing. Diamond was introduced specifically because angle-grinder attacks became common enough to need a tier above Gold. **ART** (Dutch, 1-4 stars) is a similar independent system, sometimes shown alongside Sold Secure on premium locks. When a listing cites a rating, it's worth checking which one and how recent it is — ratings do occasionally change on retest.

### Angle-Grinder Resistance

Cordless angle grinders are now cheap and widely available, and they've become the dominant urban bike-theft tool — which is why grinder-specific marketing (Litelok's composite "Barronium" material, Hiplok's D1000, coated shackles from OnGuard) has become the main selling point on premium locks. If you commute in a city with any meaningful bike-theft problem, this is the category to pay attention to rather than raw weight or shackle diameter alone.

### Weight, Key vs. Combination, and Mounting

Grinder-resistant coatings and thicker shackles add real weight — some Sold Secure Diamond locks run 1.2-2.4 kg, which matters if you're carrying it in a bag or on a belt all day rather than leaving it at a fixed dock. **Keyed locks with restricted, high-security cylinders** generally resist picking and bumping better than combination dials at the same price point; combination locks avoid the "lost key" risk but are usually the lower-security choice. Most U-locks and folding locks include a **frame-mounting bracket**; chain locks lean on belt or bag carry instead.

## A Simple Way to Decide

Start with the lock-type question: if you can usually find a proper rack or sign post, a **rigid U-lock** like the **Kryptonite Fahgettaboudit Mini (~$85-$90)** or the higher-security **ABUS Granit X-Plus 540 (~$150-$160)** gives the best security for the weight. If you regularly need to wrap both wheels, the frame, and an irregular rack, a **chain lock** like the **Hiplok Gold (~$130-$150)** is worth the extra weight. If your city has a real angle-grinder theft problem, prioritize a **Sold Secure Diamond** lock like the **Litelok X1** over a cheaper Gold-rated option, even if it costs more and weighs more. A rough industry rule of thumb: spend around 10% of your bike's value on the lock, and go Diamond-tier if that value is $1,250 or higher.

## A Few Things to Verify Before You Buy

- Confirm the **current Sold Secure or ART rating** directly with the manufacturer or Sold Secure's own site — ratings occasionally change on retest, as happened with one popular folding lock.
- Check whether the lock comes with an **anti-theft protection program** — Kryptonite's ATPO and Litelok's partner program both reimburse up to a set amount if the lock itself is defeated by force, with conditions like registering within 30 days and filing a police report promptly.
- Register your bike for free with **Bike Index** or **529 Garage** — both are nonprofit registries that meaningfully improve recovery odds if your bike is ever stolen, and neither costs anything.
- Never rely on a **cable lock alone** — treat it strictly as a secondary lock for a wheel or saddle.
- Look up the **current price directly on the retailer's page** — bike lock pricing swings noticeably with sales, and we found real discrepancies between sources during our research.
