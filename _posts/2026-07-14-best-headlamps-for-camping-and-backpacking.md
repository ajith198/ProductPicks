A headlamp is one of the smallest items in a camping or backpacking kit, and also one of the easiest to get wrong. Buy on max-lumens alone and you'll end up with something that blinds your tentmates and dies halfway through a multi-day trip, or something so dim you're squinting to read a map. The right headlamp comes down to a handful of specs that actually matter for how you camp — not the headline brightness number on the box.

This guide covers what actually matters when choosing one, plus specific models worth considering across weights and budgets. We haven't personally tested every headlamp listed here — we've researched published specs, independent lab reviews, and owner feedback, and we'll flag anywhere we're relying on that rather than hands-on testing.

## What Actually Matters in a Camping Headlamp

### Brightness — But Look at the Full Range, Not Just the Max

For general camping — setting up a tent, cooking, reading — most guides converge on **roughly 250-400 lumens** as the useful range for what you'll actually use most nights. Trail runners and anyone moving fast on technical terrain in the dark want more, often 400-1200+ lumens on a high setting. But the headline "max lumens" number on the box matters less than the **spread of brightness levels** (low/medium/high, sometimes more) — that's what lets you dial down for camp chores to save battery and dial up when you actually need to see further.

### Beam Type: Spot vs. Flood

A **spot (throw) beam** is narrow and punches out to a distance — useful for scanning the trail ahead or spotting something off in the dark. A **flood (wide) beam** lights up a broad near-field area, better for reading, cooking, or general camp tasks without a tunnel-vision effect. Most current backpacking headlamps combine both in a mixed-beam design, so you're not stuck choosing one.

### Battery Type: USB-C Rechargeable vs. AAA vs. Hybrid

**USB-C rechargeable** headlamps are convenient and often lighter, but internal lithium batteries lose capacity fast in real cold (roughly below 0°F), and you need a power bank or outlet on a multi-day trip. **AAA batteries** — specifically lithium, not alkaline — hold up much better in freezing temperatures and are available at any gas station or trail town, at the cost of extra weight if you're carrying spares. **Hybrid/dual-fuel** headlamps (Black Diamond, Petzl) run on a proprietary rechargeable pack or standard AAA batteries, giving you a fallback option — though output usually drops when running on standard batteries instead of the rechargeable cell.

### Red Light Mode

A red-light setting preserves your night vision better than white light, is less disruptive to other campers or wildlife, draws less power, and attracts fewer bugs. It's a low-cost feature that's genuinely useful and standard on nearly every headlamp in this guide.

### Weight, Water Resistance, and Runtime

Ultralight backpackers care a lot about weight — some models weigh well under 2 oz — and headband comfort (moisture-wicking material, no rear battery pack bouncing around). Water resistance is rated by IPX/IP number: **IPX4** handles splashes, **IPX7** means it survives a full dunk in a stream, and **IP68** is more submersion resistance than most campers will ever need. The number that matters most for a multi-day trip is the **runtime at low brightness**, not the headline max-lumens runtime — a headlamp might run 200+ hours on low but only 2-6 hours on its highest setting.

## A Few Things to Verify Before You Buy

- Compare **runtime at low and medium brightness**, not just the headline max-lumens figure — that's the number that actually determines whether the headlamp lasts your whole trip.
- If you're heading out in cold weather, favor a **AAA or hybrid battery model** over a rechargeable-only one — lithium-ion packs lose capacity fast below freezing.
- Double-check the **lumens figure against the specific mode** — we found inconsistent marketing between combined-mode and single-beam-mode numbers on at least one model, so compare mode-by-mode specs rather than a single headline number.
- If you're going multi-day without access to power, a **rechargeable-only headlamp** means carrying a power bank or solar charger as backup.
- Look up the **current price directly on the retailer's page** — headlamp pricing fluctuates more than you'd expect between retailers.
