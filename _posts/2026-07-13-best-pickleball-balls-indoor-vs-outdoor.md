## What Actually Matters: Indoor vs. Outdoor Balls

### Hole Count and Pattern

This is the single biggest giveaway on a package. **Outdoor balls typically have 40 smaller holes**, while **indoor balls typically have 26 larger holes**. Outdoor courts are exposed to wind, so more (smaller) holes reduce drag and keep flight predictable outside. Indoor courts have no wind to fight, so fewer, larger holes are used instead — and if you ever see a ball with an unusual hole count, check that it's actually USAPA-approved before assuming it plays like a standard ball.

### Material Hardness

Outdoor balls are molded from a **harder, thicker plastic** to survive abrasive asphalt and concrete without cracking. Indoor balls use a **softer, more flexible plastic** tuned for smooth gym floors — that softness also means indoor balls compress more on contact, which most beginners find gives a slightly quieter, more forgiving feel off the paddle.

### Seam Construction

Cheaper balls are often molded in two halves with a visible seam that can affect bounce consistency as the ball wears. Better balls (indoor and outdoor) use tighter, more precisely welded seams — worth checking reviews for if a bag of balls feels unusually erratic in testing.

### USAPA Approval

Look for **"USA Pickleball Approved"** on the packaging if you plan to play at a club, in leagues, or in any sanctioned play — non-approved balls can behave noticeably differently in weight and bounce. USA Pickleball maintains an official approved-ball list on its equipment site, and nearly every ball from an established brand (Franklin, Onix, Selkirk, Gamma, Dura) will be on it.

### Durability and Wind Resistance

Outdoor balls are the more durable of the two by design, but that durability comes at the cost of a harder impact feel. If you split time between an outdoor asphalt court and an indoor gym, know that **most players actually use outdoor balls on indoor hard courts** too (the majority of dedicated pickleball facilities have hard floors, not smooth wood) — but avoid outdoor balls on genuinely smooth wood gym floors, where they can skid and bounce inconsistently.

## A Simple Way to Decide

If you're playing mostly outdoors on asphalt or concrete, start with the **Franklin X-40 (~$9-$12 for 3)** — it's the most common ball you'll see at public courts, so practicing with it means you're training on what you'll actually face in casual games. If you're indoors on a gym or dedicated indoor court, the **Onix Fuse or GAMMA Photon indoor version (~$8-$12 for 3)** is the better starting point, since outdoor balls can feel too hard and bounce unpredictably on smooth flooring. ## A Few Things to Verify Before You Buy

- Confirm the listing specifies **indoor or outdoor** (and the hole count, 26 vs. 40) — some multi-packs mix the two, which isn't what you want if you play on one surface consistently.
- Check for **"USA Pickleball Approved"** on the packaging or listing if you plan to play in leagues, clinics, or sanctioned events.
- Buy a small pack first (3 or 6) rather than a bulk bucket until you're sure a given ball's feel and durability suit how often and where you actually play.
