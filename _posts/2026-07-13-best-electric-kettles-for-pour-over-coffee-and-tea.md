Pour-over coffee and good tea both come down to the same variable most people ignore: **water**. Too hot, and you scald delicate green tea or scorch a light-roast pour-over into bitterness. Too cool, and a black tea or dark roast never fully extracts. A basic stovetop kettle or a cheap plug-in model gives you no control over any of this — it just boils. A dedicated electric kettle built for pour-over and tea gives you a controlled pour, a controlled temperature, or often both.

This guide covers what actually matters when shopping for one, plus specific models worth considering across price points. We haven't personally brewed with every kettle listed here — we've researched published specs, pricing, and owner feedback carefully, and we'll flag anywhere we're relying on that rather than hands-on testing.

## What Actually Matters in a Pour-Over and Tea Kettle

### Gooseneck Spout vs. Standard Spout

A **gooseneck spout** is the long, narrow, curved neck you see on dedicated pour-over kettles. It restricts the water flow to a thin, steady stream, which lets you spiral-pour slowly from the center of the coffee bed outward without flooding it or blasting through the filter — the single biggest upgrade for pour-over technique. A **standard wide spout**, by contrast, pours faster and less precisely; it's fine for filling a teapot or French press but makes controlled pour-over nearly impossible. If pour-over is your main use case, a gooseneck isn't optional — it's the point of the kettle.

### Variable Temperature Control

Different teas and coffees want different water temperatures, and a few degrees make a real difference in the cup:

- **Delicate green and white teas** generally want cooler water, often in the 160-185°F range, since near-boiling water can scald them and pull out excess bitterness.
- **Oolong** typically sits in the middle, often 185-200°F.
- **Black tea and herbal infusions** usually want water at or near a full boil, around 200-212°F.
- **Pour-over coffee** is commonly brewed in the 195-205°F range, though this varies by roast — lighter roasts often benefit from slightly hotter water, darker roasts from slightly cooler.

A kettle with **precise, adjustable temperature settings** (ideally in 1-degree increments, or at minimum several labeled presets) lets you dial in the right temperature for whatever you're brewing instead of guessing or waiting for water to cool.

### Boil Speed and Wattage

**Wattage drives how fast a kettle boils.** Most dedicated gooseneck and tea kettles run 1000-1500 watts, which typically brings a liter of water to a boil in roughly 5-7 minutes — noticeably faster than a basic stovetop kettle. Higher wattage matters more if you're making multiple cups back-to-back or don't want to wait around in the morning.

### Auto Shut-Off and Keep-Warm

**Auto shut-off** turns the kettle off once water reaches temperature or boils dry, which matters for both safety and not wasting energy. **Keep-warm** functions hold water at a set temperature for a period of time (commonly 30-60 minutes) so you're not reheating between cups — useful if you're brewing more than one pour-over or pot of tea in a sitting.

### Material

**Stainless steel** is the standard for durability and heat retention, and most reputable kettles now advertise that water only contacts stainless steel (not plastic) during heating — worth confirming if that matters to you. **Glass kettles** let you watch the water heat and are common on general-purpose tea kettles, but they're less common in gooseneck pour-over designs and can be more fragile.

## A Simple Way to Decide

If pour-over coffee is the main reason you're buying a kettle, start with a **gooseneck** — the **COSORI Original (~$60-$70)** covers the fundamentals well for the price, and the **Fellow Stagg EKG (~$190-$215)** is the upgrade if you want a nicer display, a stopwatch, and finer control. If you want gooseneck precision plus finer-grained temperature adjustment than presets alone, the **Bonavita (~$80-$110)** sits in between. If tea is your priority and pour-over isn't in the picture, skip the gooseneck spout entirely and put your budget toward accurate presets — the **Hamilton Beach (~$50-$65)** or **Cuisinart PerfecTemp (~$85-$100)** both do that well.

## A Few Things to Verify Before You Buy

- Confirm whether the model you're looking at has a **gooseneck spout** if pour-over control matters to you — some listings under the same brand name are standard-spout tea kettles.
- Check the **actual temperature range and preset list** against the teas or coffee you drink most; not every kettle covers the low end (160-170°F) that delicate green and white teas want.
- Look up the **current price directly on the retailer's page** — kettle pricing across these brands fluctuates with sales more than most small kitchen appliances.
- If precise, adjustable-by-the-degree temperature matters more to you than presets, confirm the model supports that — several kettles only offer a fixed list of presets rather than full manual adjustment.
