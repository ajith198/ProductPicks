A single-serving smoothie doesn't need a full-size countertop blender taking up cabinet space and requiring you to wash a giant pitcher for one drink. A personal blender is built around a smaller jar — often one that doubles as your to-go cup — and that narrower focus is exactly what makes it convenient, as long as you pick one that can actually crush ice and frozen fruit rather than just sputtering.

This guide covers what actually matters when shopping for a personal blender, plus specific models worth considering under roughly $100. We haven't personally blended with every model listed here — we've researched published specs, pricing, and owner feedback, and we'll flag anywhere we're relying on that rather than hands-on testing.

## What Actually Matters in a Personal Blender

### Motor Wattage — and Why It's Not the Whole Story

Personal blenders range from around 200 watts on compact/portable models up to 900W+ on countertop-style single-serve blenders. As a rule of thumb, **700W or higher** reliably crushes ice and frozen fruit, while sub-300W motors are better suited to soft fruit and powders. But wattage alone doesn't tell the whole story — blade geometry and jar shape determine how well that power actually gets used, so two blenders with similar motor ratings can perform noticeably differently in practice.

### Blade Design

Stainless steel blades are standard across the category — durable, rust-resistant, and easy to clean. Several brands market an "extractor" blade design (multi-angle blades meant to process skins, seeds, and fibrous stems more thoroughly), which tends to correlate with smoother results on tougher ingredients like celery or whole nuts.

### Jar Size and the To-Go Factor

Most personal blender jars run 12-24 oz, with larger 16-32 oz cups aimed at a full breakfast-style smoothie. A wider jar opening (2 inches or more) is easier to fill and circulates ingredients better. Many personal blenders let the blending jar double as your drinking cup — you blend, swap on a screw-top lid, and walk out the door — which is a real convenience difference from cheaper models where you have to pour the blend into a separate container.

### Portability and Cordless Options

Fully cordless, USB-C rechargeable blenders (BlendJet, Oster Blend Active) let you blend without an outlet at all — genuinely useful for a car, gym bag, or office desk. The tradeoff is lower power and smaller batches than a corded model, so they're better suited to softer ingredients than heavy ice duty.

### Dishwasher-Safe Parts and Ice-Crushing

Most personal blenders advertise dishwasher-safe jars, blades, and lids, though several cordless models specifically call out that the sealing/blending lid needs hand-washing — worth checking rather than assuming every part goes in the dishwasher. Ice-crushing capability varies more than you'd expect at this price point: some low-wattage single-serve blenders explicitly aren't rated for ice, so if frozen fruit and ice are part of your routine, confirm the model is built for it.

## A Few Things to Verify Before You Buy

- Check whether the model is explicitly rated for **ice and frozen fruit** — some lower-wattage single-serve blenders aren't built for it, and that's easy to miss on a quick glance at the listing.
- If you're considering a cordless model, check the **blends-per-charge and recharge time** — these vary a lot between BlendJet and Oster, and matter if you're blending more than once a day.
- Confirm which parts are actually **dishwasher-safe** — several cordless models require hand-washing the sealing lid even if the jar and blade are dishwasher-safe.
- Blades are a **wear item** on these blenders — some manufacturers recommend replacing them roughly every six months of regular use, so factor that into the real cost of ownership.
- Look up the **current price directly on the retailer's page** — these all fluctuate with sales more than you'd expect.
